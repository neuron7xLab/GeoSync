# Claim Boundary

## What the certificate proves

For an artifact `A` that passes `generate_artifact.py verify`:

1. `A` is well-formed against `schema/artifact.schema.json`.
2. `A.tests_run` is a superset of the risk-tier-required test set, and
   (when `A.claim_status == EVIDENCE`) `A.null_models_run` is a superset
   of the risk-tier-required null-model set.
3. `set(A.null_model_results.keys()) == set(A.null_models_run)` and
   `A.metrics.hypothesis_score > max(A.null_model_results.values())`.
4. The (`A.result`, `A.test_passed`, `A.claim_status`,
   `A.claim_boundary`) tuple is internally consistent: `survived` â‡’
   `EVIDENCE` âˆ§ `VERIFIED_EVIDENCE` âˆ§ `test_passed == true`;
   `killed_with_counterexample` â‡’ `KILLED` âˆ§ `FALSIFIED_OR_REJECTED`.
5. High-risk witness rules are satisfied: `approved` status with
   structured `reviewer_id`, `review_timestamp_utc` (â‰¤30 days old at
   verification time), and `review_hash`. A `rejected` witness cannot
   declare `survived`.
6. `A.purpose.alignment_score â‰¥ 0.7` AND `A.purpose.drift_check â‰¤ 0.3`.
7. `A.external_falsification.drift_score â‰¤ 0.5` (for EVIDENCE).
8. The STN-hyperdirect ACC conflict score (max of a 7-dim vector) does
   not exceed 0.8 or the seed-deterministic adaptive threshold.
9. `A.api_version == "1.0"`.
10. `A.sha256` matches SHA-256 of canonical JSON of `A` with `sha256`
    removed.

## What the certificate does NOT prove

1. That the caller actually ran the tests listed in `A.tests_run`.
2. That `A.metrics.hypothesis_score` was computed correctly from the
   underlying data.
3. That `A.null_model_results` values were generated by real null
   models rather than fabricated.
4. That `A.reproducibility.prompt_hash` is the SHA-256 of the actual
   prompt used.
5. That `A.reproducibility.environment.requirements_lock_sha256`
   matches an actual `requirements.lock`.
6. That `A.external_falsification.observation_hash` corresponds to a
   real observation from `A.external_falsification.substrate`.
7. That `A.witness.review_hash` was computed from a real review
   document or that the reviewer named in `A.witness.reviewer_id`
   exists.
8. That a system relying on `A` is itself well-engineered, safe, or
   correct.

## What this means operationally

The IEV is a **certificate format and structural gate**, not a test
runner or a truth oracle. The truth of caller-supplied numerics and
hashes is an external obligation that lives upstream of this module
(in the runner that actually executes tests, computes null
distributions, observes the substrate, and authors the review).

An attacker who controls the caller can produce IEV-valid artifacts
with fabricated numerics; the certificate then proves only that the
attacker's claims are mutually consistent and survive the structural
contract â€” not that they correspond to reality. Detecting upstream
fabrication is the responsibility of the witness, the
`requirements_lock_sha256` chain of custody, and any external audit
that compares `observation_hash` to its substrate.

- "This artifact survived â†’ the hypothesis is true."
- "This artifact is signed â†’ the underlying experiment was honest."
- "This artifact has EVIDENCE â†’ it is safe to act on."

The correct interpretation of a survived artifact is:

> *Under the caller's claimed numerics, the structural contract holds.
> Any belief that the numerics correspond to reality must be supported
> elsewhere.*
